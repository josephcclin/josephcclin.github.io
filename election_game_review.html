  7. It seems to me that parties rather ignore any utility contribution that their supporters get from other winning parties. Anyway, if the party loses they are not going to care about the utility of their candidate. Otherwise, if the party wins, the party wants have their own electorate as happy as possible. Assuming the proposed payoff, it seems to me that it might be beneficial for a party, to nominate its epsilon-worse candidate, if for the price of this epsilon utility loss, the party's supporters get more than epsilon utility in expectancy (this appear to could happen if the summation terms coming from other parties' nominees give a value bigger than something like epsilon^2). This, however, does not look as a strategically-correct nomination. So, it seems a more natural payoff would be simply the probability that the party's supporters get multiplied by the utility of the party's nominee for the party's supporters. Summarizing, I am not convinced by this choice of function (maybe I am missing something obvious?).

    8. => We regard a party, which consists of its supporters, as a collective concept. A candidate will be nominated by the supporters of the party to compete in an election campaign. Supporters of a party can benefit not only from the candidate nominated for them but also from that from the competing party. Since the winning candidate serves for the whole society, that is, for all the voters, not only for its supporters, we consider the payoff of the party as the expected utility the supporters can get to well formulate the payoff whenever the party wins or loses.

  8. Why are egoistic games “natural and reasonable” in the real world? I am not so sure that it is natural that even more extremist candidate from the party a voter supports is indeed preferred to all representatives of other parties. To me it only works in quite polarized party settings and might not be true when there are many parties. Hence, this assumption seem to already favor the thesis of the paper that the paper's results support the Duverger's law.

    9. => As a party is a collective concept which consists of its supporters, a candidate of the party is expected to be more favorable than the one of the competing party for the supporters. Although it seems to be natural, it is not always the case in real world. For example, a smaller party that has fewer supporters and less resource is difficult to compete with a larger party, so a candidate of the larger party is possibly more beneficial to the supporters of the smaller party than that of a smaller party.
