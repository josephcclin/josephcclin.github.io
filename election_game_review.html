  5. It seems to me that parties rather ignore any utility contribution that their supporters get from other winning parties. Anyway, if the party loses they are not going to care about the utility of their candidate. Otherwise, if the party wins, the party wants have their own electorate as happy as possible. Assuming the proposed payoff, it seems to me that it might be beneficial for a party, to nominate its epsilon-worse candidate, if for the price of this epsilon utility loss, the party's supporters get more than epsilon utility in expectancy (this appear to could happen if the summation terms coming from other parties' nominees give a value bigger than something like epsilon^2). This, however, does not look as a strategically-correct nomination. So, it seems a more natural payoff would be simply the probability that the party's supporters get multiplied by the utility of the party's nominee for the party's supporters. Summarizing, I am not convinced by this choice of function (maybe I am missing something obvious?).
